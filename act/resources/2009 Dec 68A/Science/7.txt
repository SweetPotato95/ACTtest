<br>
<div class = "para" style = "font-size: 16;font-family:Microsoft YAHEI;">A physical pendulum is an object that is suspended vertically from a rigid support (see example in Figure 1).</div>

<br>
<div class = "para" style = "font-size: 16;font-family:Microsoft YAHEI;"><img src='file:\$$$\resources\2009 Dec 68A\imgs\261.png' width = '2052'height = '1110'/></div>

<br>
<div class = "para" style = "font-size: 16;font-family:Microsoft YAHEI;">Figure 1</div>

<br>
<div class = "para" style = "font-size: 16;font-family:Microsoft YAHEI;">L is the distance between the rod (the rigid support) and the pendulum's center of mass (CM). When the pendulum is displaced from its equilibrium position and then released, it oscillates (swings back and forth) about the rod, which acts as the axis of rotation.</div>

<br>
<div class = "para" style = "font-size: 16;font-family:Microsoft YAHEI;">Students tested several types of physical pendulums. In each trial, first, they computed the moment of inertia, I, in kilogram meters<sup>2</sup> (kg m<sup>2</sup>), of a pendulum. (I is a measure of the mass of a pendulum and how the mass is distributed.) Next, they displaced the pendulum slightly from its equilibrium position, released it, and measured the time, in seconds, it took the pendulum to oscillate through 20 cycles (1 cycle is 1 round-trip back and forth). Finally, they computed the pendulum's average period, P (the average time it took to complete 1 cycle).</div>
