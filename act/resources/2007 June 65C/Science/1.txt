
<div class = "para" style = "font-size: 16;font-family:Microsoft YAHEI;">Some students conducted 4 studies using slidewire ganerators of various sizes. Each slidewire generator contained a U-shaped electrical conductor (USEC), a movable conducting rod of length L that connected the sides of the USEC, and an ammeter (A). All conductors were made of the same metal. Using a motor-driven shaft attached to the conducting rod, the students could move the rod either to the left or to the right ac speed v (see Figure l).</div>

<div class = "para" style = "font-size: 16;font-family:Microsoft YAHEI;"><img src='file:\$$$\resources\2007 June 65C\imgs\213.png' width = '497'height = '368'/></div>

<br>
<div class = "para" style = "font-size: 16;font-family:Microsoft YAHEI;">In each trial, the slidewire generator was placed perpendicular to a magnetic field of strength B that pointed vertically toward the ceiling. The field was constant in strength and direction. When the rod was moved; an amount of electrical current, I, was generated in the circuit made by the USEC and the conducting rod (see Figure 2).</div>

<div class = "para" style = "font-size: 16;font-family:Microsoft YAHEI;"><img src='file:\$$$\resources\2007 June 65C\imgs\214.png' width = '601'height = '970'/></div>

<br>
<div class = "para" style = "font-size: 16;font-family:Microsoft YAHEI;">I was positive when the current moved clockwise through the circuit and negative when the current moved counterclockwise through the circuit.</div>
