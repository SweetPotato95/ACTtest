
<div class = "para" style = "font-size: 16;font-family:Microsoft YAHEI;">﻿A science class studied the frictional forces involving moving surfaces.</div>

<br>
<div class = "para" style = "font-size: 16;font-family:Microsoft YAHEI;">In each of the following experiments, the students used identical bricks that were either unwrapped or that were securely wrapped in paper. The students tied a string around a brick, fastened a spring scale to the string, and pulled the brick across a table, as shown in Figure 1.</div>

<br>
<div class = "para" style = "font-size: 16;font-family:Microsoft YAHEI;"><img src='file:\$$$\resources\2007 Dec 65E\imgs\169.png' width = '723'height = '191'/></div>

<div class = "para" style = "font-size: 16;font-family:Microsoft YAHEI;">Figure 1</div>

<br>
<div class = "para" style = "font-size: 16;font-family:Microsoft YAHEI;">The amount of the force, in newtons (N), required to move the brick across the table at a constant speed was measured on the spring scale. This value equaled the amount of the frictional force between the brick and the tabletop.</div>

<br>
<div class = "para" style = "font-size: 16;font-family:Microsoft YAHEI;">(Note: A brick's facers consist of 2 faces, 2 sides, and 2 ends, as shown in Figure 2. Unless otherwise stated, each brick lay on one of its faces. Also, unless otherwise stated, all bricks were pulled across the table at the same low speed.)</div>
