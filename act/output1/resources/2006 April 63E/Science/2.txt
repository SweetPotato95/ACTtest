<div class = "para" style = "font-size: 16;font-family:Microsoft YAHEI;">Oxyfluorocarbons (OFCs) are iols composed of only carbon (C), fluorine (F), and oxygen (O). They are useful as lubricants in exposed mechanical systems on spacecraft. However, OFCs tend to decompose upon exporure to heated metal surfaces, which usually have a metal oxide (MO) outer layer (see Figure 1).</div>

<div class = "para" style = "font-size: 16;font-family:Microsoft YAHEI;"><img src='file:\$$$\resources\2006 April 63E\imgs\94.png' width = '730'height = '292'/></div>

<br>
<div class = "para" style = "font-size: 16;font-family:Microsoft YAHEI;">When an oil decomposes, the mechanical systems stop working, crippling the spacecraft.</div>

<br>
<div class = "para" style = "font-size: 16;font-family:Microsoft YAHEI;">Chemists studied the decomposition of 2 OFCs (OFC1 and OFC2).</div>

<br>
<div class = "para" style = "font-size: 16;font-family:Microsoft YAHEI;">Experiment 1</div>

<br>
<div class = "para" style = "font-size: 16;font-family:Microsoft YAHEI;">In a chamber at 100℃ and 0.01 atmospheres (atm) pressure, 1.5 g of OFC1 was mixed with 50 mg of powdered iron (Ⅲ) oxide (<img src='file:\$$$\resources\2006 April 63E\imgs\95.png' width = '72'height = '42'/>), an MO. The gases produced by the decomposition of the OFC caused the pressure to increase. The induction period (the time for the pressure to rise to 0.20 atm) was measured. Shorter induction periods indicate more rapid decomposition. The procedure was repented at different temperatures, and for OFC2 (see Table 1).</div>
